Week 6: Molecular Genetics

Genes Control Inheritance

DNA is the genetic material that carries instructions for traits. Genes are segments of DNA located on chromosomes.
  • DNA — the molecule that stores and transmits genetic information
  • Genes are located on chromosomes and serve as the instructions for building and maintaining an organism
  • Genes determine traits (observable characteristics) by encoding specific proteins
  • The passing of genes from parents to offspring is the basis of inheritance

DNA Stores Genetic Information as Nucleotide Sequences

DNA is a nucleic acid composed of two strands twisted into a double helix, made up of four nucleotides: A, T, G, C.
  • DNA stands for deoxyribonucleic acid
  • DNA is a nucleic acid — a polymer made of nucleotide monomers
  • Two strands twist around each other forming a double helix
  • Four nucleotide bases: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C)
  • The entire set of DNA in an organism = its genome

DNA’s Structure Explains Function

  • The sequence (order) of bases along a DNA strand stores tremendous amounts of information
  • Different species and individuals have different sequences → this produces genetic variation
  • Even small changes in base sequence can alter the function of a gene
  • The information in DNA directs the production of proteins, which carry out cellular functions

DNA Is Built from Two Helically Wound Polynucleotides

Each nucleotide = phosphate group + sugar (deoxyribose) + nitrogenous base. Base-pairing rules: A pairs with T, G pairs with C.
Nucleotide ComponentDescription
Phosphate groupLinks nucleotides together in the sugar-phosphate backbone
Deoxyribose sugarFive-carbon sugar; connects phosphate to nitrogenous base
Nitrogenous baseOne of four: A, T, G, or C; carries the genetic code

Base-Pairing Rules (Watson-Crick)

  • Adenine (A) always pairs with Thymine (T) — 2 hydrogen bonds
  • Guanine (G) always pairs with Cytosine (C) — 3 hydrogen bonds
  • The two strands are complementary — knowing one strand’s sequence tells you the other
  • The strands run in antiparallel directions (5′→3′ and 3′→5′)
Memorize base-pairing rules: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds). Very testable!

The Three-Dimensional Structure of DNA

  • Watson and Crick discovered that DNA is a double helix (Nobel Prize 1962)
  • Two antiparallel polynucleotide strands wound around each other
  • Held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs
  • The sugar-phosphate backbone is on the outside; bases face inward like rungs of a ladder
  • Rosalind Franklin’s X-ray crystallography work was critical to this discovery

Different Sets of Genes Are Expressed in Different Cell Types

All cells in a multicellular organism contain the same DNA, but not all genes are active in every cell type.
  • Every cell in your body has the same genome
  • Only certain genes are “turned on” (expressed) in each cell type — this is gene expression
  • Promoters act as on/off switches for transcription of specific genes
  • Development is regulated by changes in gene expression over time
  • A muscle cell expresses different genes than a nerve cell, even though both have the same DNA
Gene expression = which genes are turned on in a particular cell at a particular time. This is how one genome can produce many different cell types.

Most Genes Code for Proteins, Which Generate Phenotypes

Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein. A gene is a segment of DNA coding for at least one protein.
ProcessDescriptionLocation
TranscriptionDNA is copied into mRNA (messenger RNA)Nucleus
TranslationRibosomes read mRNA and build a proteinRibosomes (cytoplasm)
  • mRNA (messenger RNA) is a single-stranded nucleic acid similar to DNA that carries instructions from DNA to ribosomes
  • The reading of mRNA’s DNA-based sequence to build a protein is called translation
  • Each protein has a unique amino acid sequence that determines its shape and function
  • A protein’s function produces a specific phenotype (observable trait)
  • Gene expression = manifestation of a specific phenotype from a gene
  • Simplified: 1 gene = 1 protein (though some genes code for RNA molecules)
Know the central dogma: DNA → (transcription) → RNA → (translation) → Protein. This flow of information is fundamental!

How DNA Is Replicated

Semiconservative replication: each parent strand serves as a template for a new complementary strand.
  • The double helix unwinds and each strand serves as a template
  • DNA polymerase is the key enzyme that builds the new strand by adding complementary nucleotides
  • A dozen or more enzymes are involved in the full replication process
  • Each resulting double helix has one old (parent) strand + one new (daughter) strand = semiconservative
  • One parent helix produces two identical daughter helices

Why “Semiconservative”?

Each new DNA molecule conserves (keeps) one strand from the parent and builds one entirely new strand. This was proven by the Meselson-Stahl experiment.

Repairing Replication Errors and Damaged DNA

  • Human cells copy ~6 billion base pairs each time they divide
  • DNA polymerase proofreads as it goes, correcting most errors during replication
  • Additional repair enzymes correct about 99% of remaining mismatches
  • Uncorrected changes in the DNA sequence = mutations
  • Despite repair mechanisms, some errors slip through — these drive genetic variation

Few Mistakes Are Made in DNA Replication

  • Mutagens = substances or energy sources that cause mutations (e.g., UV radiation, chemicals, X-rays)
  • Most mutations are neutral (no effect on the organism)
  • Some mutations are harmful — can cause diseases like Huntington’s disease
  • Rarely, mutations can be beneficial (provide an advantage)
  • Mutations are the ultimate source of genetic variation, driving evolution
Mutagens cause mutations, but remember: most mutations are neutral, not harmful. Mutations drive genetic variation and evolution.

Week 7: Heredity

Reviewing Genetic Information

Genetic information = DNA (the genome); it is passed to progeny cells via mitotic division or to offspring via meiotic gamete production.
  • Genetics — the study of how genetic information (DNA) is organized, replicated, and passed on
  • Heredity — specifically how genetic information is passed to offspring through gametes (sperm & egg)
  • Information flows from DNA → RNA → Protein (the central dogma)
  • All of an organism’s DNA = its genome

Essential Terms in Genetics

TermDefinition
Genetic traitAny inherited characteristic that can be observed or detected (e.g., eye color, blood type)
Invariant genetic traitA trait that is the same in all individuals of a population (e.g., having two eyes)
PhenotypeThe observable display of a particular version of a genetic trait (what you see)
GenotypeThe genes (allele combination) that underlie a given phenotype (what the DNA says)
Phenotype = Physical appearance. Genotype = Genetic makeup. The genotype determines the phenotype.

Diploid Cells Have Two Copies of Every Gene

  • Somatic cells (body cells) are diploid (2n) — they contain homologous pairs of chromosomes
  • Each homologous pair has one chromosome from the father (paternal) and one from the mother (maternal)
  • Humans: 23 pairs = 46 chromosomes
  • Gametes (sex cells: sperm & egg) are haploid (n) with one set of 23 chromosomes, produced by meiosis
  • Each gene occupies a specific location on a chromosome called a locus (plural: loci)

Genotype Directs Phenotype

Alleles are different versions of a given gene. They arise from mutations and are the basis of genetic diversity.
  • Because diploid cells have two copies of each gene, an individual has two alleles for every gene (one on each homologous chromosome)
  • Homozygous — two copies of the same allele (e.g., BB or bb)
  • Heterozygous — two different alleles (e.g., Bb)
  • The specific combination of alleles (genotype) determines the observable trait (phenotype)

Dominant and Recessive Alleles

TermDefinitionExample
Dominant alleleControls the phenotype when paired with a different allele; written as uppercase letter (e.g., B)Bb shows the dominant phenotype
Recessive alleleHas no visible effect on phenotype when paired with a dominant allele; written lowercase (e.g., b)Only bb shows recessive phenotype
  • An individual must have two copies of a recessive allele (homozygous recessive, bb) to display the recessive phenotype
  • A heterozygote (Bb) shows the dominant phenotype but carries the recessive allele
  • Some traits are controlled by more than one gene — these are polygenic traits
Know the difference between homozygous dominant (BB), heterozygous (Bb), and homozygous recessive (bb) — very testable!

Gene Mutations

A mutation is a change in the DNA nucleotide sequence that creates new alleles.
  • Mutations are the ultimate source of all new genetic variation
  • Most mutations are neutral (no effect on phenotype)
  • Some mutations are harmful — harmful mutations are often recessive
  • Rarely, mutations can be beneficial (provide an advantage in a given environment)
  • Harmful recessive mutations can be masked by the dominant allele in heterozygotes
  • Carriers — heterozygous individuals who carry one copy of a harmful recessive allele without showing symptoms
  • Mutations occur at random and are not directed by need
  • Only mutations in gametes (sex cells) are heritable — somatic mutations are not passed to offspring
Remember: Only gamete mutations can be inherited by offspring!

Controlled Crosses / Genetic Crosses

  • P generation (Parental) — the original parents in a cross
  • F1 generation (First filial) — offspring of the P generation
  • F2 generation (Second filial) — offspring produced by crossing F1 individuals with each other
  • Controlled mating experiments allow scientists to study inheritance patterns

Mendel’s Contributions

  • Before Mendel: the blending hypothesis suggested offspring had intermediate phenotypes of both parents
  • Gregor Mendel (1860s) proposed that inheritance involves two separate units (genes), one from each parent — they do NOT blend
  • He observed a 3:1 ratio of dominant to recessive phenotypes in the F2 generation
  • Mendel used true-breeding (purebred/homozygous) pea plants for his crosses
  • Studied 7 traits in pea plants: seed shape, seed color, pod shape, pod color, flower color, flower position, stem height
True-breeding = homozygous for a trait (BB or bb). When crossed with themselves, they always produce offspring with the same phenotype.

Mendel’s 5 Key Points

  1. Alternative versions of genes (alleles) cause variation in inherited characters
  2. Offspring inherit one copy of each gene from each parent (two alleles total)
  3. A dominant allele has exclusive control over phenotype when paired with a recessive allele
  4. Two alleles segregate during meiosis into different gametes (each gamete gets one allele)
  5. Gametes fuse randomly during fertilization

Mendel’s Laws

Law of Segregation

The two alleles for each gene separate (segregate) during meiosis, so each gamete receives only ONE allele.
  • Applies to single-trait (monohybrid) crosses
  • A monohybrid cross of two heterozygotes (Bb × Bb) produces a 3:1 phenotypic ratio and a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio (BB : Bb : bb)

Law of Independent Assortment

Alleles of different genes on different chromosomes segregate independently of each other during meiosis.
  • Applies to two-trait (dihybrid) crosses
  • A dihybrid cross of two heterozygotes (BbRr × BbRr) produces a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio
  • Punnett squares are used to predict the outcomes of crosses
  • This law only applies when genes are on different chromosomes (not linked)
Know both laws! Segregation = one gene, two alleles separate. Independent Assortment = two genes on different chromosomes sort independently.

Probability in Genetics

Mendel used probability to analyze data from his crosses and deduce the patterns of inheritance.
  • We can predict the probability that a particular offspring will have a certain phenotype or genotype
  • However, we cannot predict the actual phenotype or genotype of any individual offspring — only the likelihood
  • The probability of a particular phenotype is completely unaffected by the number of offspring already born
  • Each fertilization event is independent — previous offspring do not influence the next (like flipping a coin)
  • Example: If Bb × Bb gives a 25% chance of bb, that probability stays 25% for EVERY child, regardless of how many bb children have already been born
This is the gambler’s fallacy concept: past outcomes do NOT change future probabilities. Each offspring is an independent event.

Crossing Over — A Major Source of Genetic Variation

Crossing over is the exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during Prophase I of meiosis. It produces recombinant chromosomes with new allele combinations.
  • During Prophase I, homologous chromosomes pair up tightly in a process called synapsis
  • The paired homologs form a tetrad (4 chromatids total — 2 from each homolog)
  • Non-sister chromatids (one from each homolog) physically cross over and swap segments at points called chiasmata (singular: chiasma)
  • The result: recombinant chromosomes that carry new combinations of alleles not found in either parent
  • Without crossing over, offspring would only receive intact parental chromosomes — crossing over dramatically increases genetic diversity

Linked Genes and Crossing Over

  • Genes on the same chromosome are called linked genes — they tend to be inherited together
  • Crossing over is the ONLY way to separate linked genes and create new allele combinations
  • The closer two genes are on a chromosome, the less likely crossing over will occur between them
  • The farther apart two genes are, the more likely crossing over will separate them
  • This principle is used to create genetic maps (linkage maps) showing gene distances
Crossing over is a VERY testable topic! Know: when it occurs (Prophase I), what it does (exchanges segments between non-sister chromatids), and why it matters (increases genetic variation, separates linked genes).

Using Punnett Squares

  • Place one parent’s alleles across the top, the other down the side
  • Fill in each box by combining the alleles from the row and column
  • Monohybrid example: Bb × Bb → 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb (genotypic); 3 dominant : 1 recessive (phenotypic)
  • Dihybrid example: BbRr × BbRr uses a 4×4 grid (16 boxes) → 9:3:3:1 ratio
  • A test cross (unknown × homozygous recessive) is used to determine if an individual with a dominant phenotype is BB or Bb

Extensions of Mendel’s Laws

Mendel’s work was based on traits controlled by a single gene with a dominant and a recessive allele (complete dominance). Real inheritance patterns are often more complex.

Complete Dominance

Complete dominance = the dominant allele fully masks the recessive allele, so the heterozygote looks identical to the homozygous dominant.
  • Assumption in Mendelian genetics: one gene = one trait
  • Examples of complete dominance in humans: widow’s peak, free earlobes, dimples
  • Most traits are actually the result of genetic interactions — complete dominance is the simple case

Incomplete Dominance

The heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype between the two homozygotes — neither allele is fully dominant.
  • Example: Red flower (RR) × White flower (WW) → F1 = Pink flowers (RW)
  • F2 ratio: 1 Red : 2 Pink : 1 White (1:2:1 phenotypic AND genotypic)
  • The heterozygote phenotype is a blend of both homozygote phenotypes

Codominance

Both alleles are equally and fully expressed in the heterozygote — neither is dominant over the other.
  • Example: ABO blood types — IA and IB alleles are codominant
  • An IAIB individual has type AB blood (both A and B antigens present)
  • Unlike incomplete dominance (which produces a blend), codominance produces both phenotypes simultaneously

Incomplete Dominance vs. Codominance

FeatureIncomplete DominanceCodominance
Heterozygote phenotypeIntermediate (blend)Both phenotypes visible simultaneously
Classic exampleRed × White = Pink flowersType A × Type B = Type AB blood
F2 phenotypic ratio1:2:11:2:1

Pleiotropy

A single gene affects multiple (seemingly unrelated) traits.
  • Example: Albinism — a single gene mutation affects skin color, hair color, AND eye color
  • Example: Sickle cell disease — one gene mutation affects red blood cell shape, oxygen transport, and multiple organs

Epistasis

Alleles of one gene alter or mask the effects of another gene.
  • Example: Coat color in Labrador retrievers — the E gene determines whether pigment is deposited; the B gene determines pigment color
  • ee genotype = yellow coat regardless of BB, Bb, or bb
  • Epistasis can modify standard Mendelian ratios (e.g., 9:3:4 instead of 9:3:3:1)

Gene Interactions in Cancer: The Wnt/APC Pathway

The APC protein is a tumor suppressor involved in the Wnt signaling pathway. APC normally targets β-catenin for destruction. When APC is mutated, β-catenin accumulates and activates genes that promote cell growth → cancer. This is an example of how epistatic gene interactions (one gene product controlling another’s effect) can have life-or-death consequences.

Environment and Gene Expression

  • The environment can alter gene expression without changing the DNA sequence
  • Example: Siamese cats — a temperature-sensitive allele produces darker fur on cooler body extremities (ears, paws, tail, face)
  • Example: Hydrangea flower color changes with soil pH
  • Phenotype = Genotype + Environment

Polygenic and Complex Traits

Polygenic traits are governed by more than one gene — each gene contributes a small amount to the phenotype.
  • Examples: skin color, running speed, blood pressure, body size, height
  • 12+ genes control melanin production and skin color
  • Polygenic traits show continuous variation — a smooth, graded range of phenotypic classes (bell curve distribution)
  • Complex traits cannot be predicted by Mendel’s laws alone
  • Both genetic and environmental factors contribute to complex traits
  • Phenotypic diversity is an evolutionary benefit — it provides more raw material for natural selection

Key Comparison: Mendelian vs. Complex Inheritance

Week 8: Hereditary Disease & Cancer Genetics

Human Genetic Disorders

Studying genetics leads to the prevention and cure of hereditary diseases. However, human genetics is harder to study than model organisms.
  • Understanding inheritance patterns helps with prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of genetic diseases
  • Challenges of studying human genetics:
    • Long generation time (decades, not days)
    • Variable mate selection (cannot perform controlled crosses)
    • Small family sizes (limited offspring data)

Pedigrees

A pedigree is a chart showing genetic relationships and inheritance patterns over two or more generations.
SymbolMeaning
○ (Circle)Female
□ (Square)Male
Filled shape (● / ■)Affected individual
Half-filled shapeCarrier (heterozygous)

  • Geneticists use pedigrees to trace inheritance patterns when controlled crosses are impossible
  • Pedigrees reveal whether a trait is dominant or recessive, autosomal or sex-linked
Key pedigree clue: If two unaffected parents have an affected child, the trait is almost certainly autosomal recessive (both parents are carriers).

How to Identify Inheritance Patterns from Pedigrees

This is a CRITICAL exam skill — you must be able to look at a pedigree and determine the inheritance pattern!

Autosomal Recessive Pedigree Pattern

In autosomal recessive inheritance, an individual must be homozygous recessive (aa) to show the trait. Carriers (Aa) are unaffected.

How to recognize autosomal recessive:

  • Two unaffected parents can have affected children — this is the #1 giveaway! Both parents must be carriers (Aa × Aa)
  • The trait often skips generations — it can appear in grandchildren even if no parent shows it
  • Affected individuals are usually the children of two unaffected carrier parents
  • Males and females are affected equally (not sex-linked)
  • When two carriers mate: 25% affected, 50% carriers, 25% unaffected
  • Affected × carrier = 50% affected, 50% carrier
  • Affected × affected = 100% affected
  • Examples: cystic fibrosis, sickle cell, PKU, Tay-Sachs, albinism
Quick test: If both parents are UNAFFECTED but have an AFFECTED child → autosomal recessive. This pattern CANNOT happen with dominant inheritance.

Autosomal Dominant Pedigree Pattern

In autosomal dominant inheritance, only ONE copy of the mutant allele (Aa or AA) is needed to show the trait.

How to recognize autosomal dominant:

  • Every affected individual has at least one affected parent — the trait does NOT skip generations
  • The trait appears in every generation in a direct line
  • Unaffected individuals cannot pass the trait to their children (they are homozygous normal, aa)
  • Males and females are affected equally
  • An affected heterozygote (Aa) × unaffected (aa) = 50% affected, 50% unaffected
  • If two unaffected parents have a child, that child CANNOT be affected (key difference from recessive!)
  • Examples: Huntington’s disease, achondroplasia (dwarfism), polydactyly
Quick test: If EVERY affected person has at least one AFFECTED parent → autosomal dominant. The trait never skips a generation.

Sources of Inherited Genetic Disorders

  • Somatic mutations — occur in body cells; affect only the individual; NOT passed to offspring
  • Gamete mutations — occur in sex cells (sperm/egg); CAN be passed to offspring
  • Two sources of inherited disorders:
    1. Mutations in individual genes (single-gene disorders)
    2. Abnormalities in chromosome number or structure (chromosomal disorders)
Only gamete mutations are heritable! Somatic mutations (like most cancers) are NOT passed to children.

Autosomal Recessive Disorders

  • Several thousand known autosomal recessive disorders
  • Must inherit TWO copies of the defective allele to be affected (homozygous recessive)
  • Heterozygous parents = carriers (unaffected but can pass allele to children)
  • Often skip generations — 75% of carrier × carrier offspring appear unaffected (3 out of 4)
  • Lethal recessive disorders persist in populations because carriers are unharmed

Key Examples

The slides list these as examples of autosomal recessive disorders. Know the inheritance pattern (two copies of defective allele needed, carriers are heterozygous).
  • Cystic fibrosis — autosomal recessive disorder (example from slides)
  • Sickle cell disease — autosomal recessive disorder
  • PKU (Phenylketonuria) — autosomal recessive disorder
  • Tay-Sachs disease — autosomal recessive disorder

Autosomal Dominant Disorders

  • Less common for serious diseases (affected individuals often cannot reproduce)
  • Only ONE copy of the defective allele needed — affected parent has 50% chance of passing to each child
  • Dominant disorders that prevent reproduction are rare and often arise from new mutations

Key Example: Huntington’s Disease

  • Huntington’s disease is a dominant genetic disorder that expresses its effects after childbearing age
  • A parent with Huntington’s has a 50% chance of passing it to each child
  • Dominant disorders that prevent reproduction are uncommon and most often appear as new mutations
Huntington’s persists in the population because it expresses its effects AFTER childbearing age — people pass it on before knowing they have it.

Sex-Linked Inheritance

Sex-linked genes are located on the X or Y chromosome. Males are hemizygous for X-linked genes (only one copy).
  • Males: one X + one Y; Females: two X chromosomes
  • ~50 genes on the Y chromosome; ~1,100 genes on the X chromosome
  • Males cannot be heterozygous for X-linked genes — they are hemizygous (one allele only)
  • Males are more likely to show X-linked recessive disorders (only need one copy)
  • Females need TWO copies of a recessive X-linked allele to be affected
  • Carrier females (XAXa) are unaffected but can pass the allele to sons

X-Linked Recessive Examples

The slides list these as examples of X-linked recessive disorders. Know the inheritance pattern (males only need one copy, females need two).
  • Red-green color blindness — X-linked recessive
  • Hemophilia — X-linked recessive
  • Duchenne muscular dystrophy — X-linked recessive
Pattern: An unaffected carrier mother can have affected sons (50% chance) but daughters are usually unaffected (carriers at most).

Chromosomal Abnormalities

Changes in chromosome number or structure. Most large chromosomal changes lead to embryo death.

Types of Structural Changes

TypeWhat Happens
DeletionA piece of a chromosome breaks off and is lost
InversionA fragment breaks off and returns to the correct place on the original chromosome, but with the genetic loci in reverse order
TranslocationA broken piece attaches to a different (nonhomologous) chromosome
DuplicationA chromosome becomes longer because it ends up with two copies of a particular chromosome fragment
Remember DITD: Deletion, Inversion, Translocation, Duplication — the four types of chromosomal structural changes.

Changes in Chromosome Number

Nondisjunction = failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis, leading to abnormal chromosome numbers.
  • Nondisjunction can occur in meiosis I or meiosis II
  • Results in gametes with too many or too few chromosomes

Key Examples

ConditionChromosomes
Down syndromeTrisomy 21 — three copies of chromosome 21 (caused by nondisjunction)
Changes in the number of sex chromosomes have more minor effects than changes in autosomal chromosome number.
